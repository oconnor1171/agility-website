Restaurant Struggles Are Real

Expert Solutions from Robert O'Connor Jr., CPA

By Robert O'Connor Jr., CPA

Running a restaurant is one of the most challenging businesses. You're managing food costs, labor, customer satisfaction, and a thousand other details every single day. If you're struggling, you're not alone. Here are the common challenges I see restaurant owners facing and what you can do about them.

Managing Cash Flow

Cash flow is the lifeblood of your restaurant. You have daily expenses—labor, food, utilities—but your revenue can be unpredictable. Some days are slow, some are busy. You need cash on hand to cover your bills even when business is slow.

The key is to plan ahead. Understand your seasonal patterns. Most restaurants are slower in certain months. Plan for this. Build cash reserves during your busy months to cover the slow months. Track your cash daily. Know exactly how much cash you have available.

Consider vendor terms. Can you negotiate 30-day payment terms instead of paying cash? Can you offer discounts for early payment to bring in cash faster? These small adjustments can make a huge difference in your cash flow.

Labor Shortages

The restaurant industry faces a serious labor shortage. Good staff is hard to find and harder to keep. But labor is one of your biggest expenses, often 30% or more of revenue.

You need to be strategic about staffing. Schedule carefully based on expected business. Cross-train staff so they can fill multiple roles. Invest in training and development so employees want to stay. A high turnover rate is expensive—it costs money and time to recruit, train, and get new people productive.

Look at your labor as an investment in your business. Paying slightly more for reliable, trained staff can actually save you money compared to the costs of constantly training new people. And keep track of your tip reporting and compliance—this is critical with your staff.

Competing in a Saturated Market

In most cities, there are too many restaurants competing for customers. How do you stand out? You can't compete on price alone—that's a race to the bottom.

Instead, focus on what makes you unique. Is it your food quality? Your customer service? Your unique concept? Your location? Build your brand around that. Use social media. Create a loyal customer base. Get people talking about your restaurant.

And understand your financials. Know which menu items are most profitable. Focus on selling the items that make you the most money. Track your customer counts. Know your average check size. These metrics tell you if your strategies are working.

Controlling Rising Costs

Food costs, labor costs, rent, utilities—everything is going up. You can't just raise your prices without losing customers. You need to be smarter about costs.

First, analyze your food costs. Track your inventory carefully. Identify waste. Can you adjust your menu to use cheaper ingredients? Can you reduce portion sizes slightly? Can you eliminate low-profit items from your menu?

Look at all your expenses. Are you getting the best prices on your supplies? Can you negotiate better vendor terms? Are there cost-saving opportunities in your operations? Maybe you're over-staffed during slow periods. Maybe your utilities are higher than they need to be.

The key is to track everything. You can't manage what you don't measure. Create a system to track your costs by category. Review these numbers regularly. Look for trends. Look for opportunities to improve.
